Consent vs DNC and exemptions

Not every marketing message needs a register check, and not every message even falls under the Do Not Call rules at all. Two things decide it: whether you have consent, and whether the message is exempt. Get these two right and half your marketing questions answer themselves. This session gives leaders the shape of both, so you can tell your team which sends are safe, which need a check, and which are out of scope entirely.

Part 0

Why consent and exemptions come first

Session a3 gave you the DNC layer: three registers, check before you send. But two questions sit in front of that check every time. First, do you already have the person's consent - because consent lets you message even a listed number. Second, is this message even a marketing ("specified") message - because if it is exempt, the DNC rules do not apply at all. A leader who can hold these two ideas apart stops asking the compliance team about every send and starts asking only about the ones that actually carry risk.

Part 1 · covers consent and the register check

Consent: the shortcut and the standard 9 min live

There are two lawful ways to land a marketing message on a Singapore number. One is to check the register and confirm the number is not listed. The other is to have the person's consent - which lets you message them even if the number is on the register. Consent is the shortcut, but only if it meets the standard.

LiveWhat "clear and unambiguous" consent actually means3 min▶

Consent is not a mood or an assumption. To rely on it, you need consent that is clear and unambiguous and captured in written or otherwise accessible form - something you could show the PDPC later. The test is simple to state and easy to fail.

  • It requires a positive action - the person actively opts in. Ticking a box themselves, replying "yes", signing up on purpose.
  • Silence and inaction are NOT consent. A pre-ticked box, a "we will assume you agree unless you tell us otherwise", or a failure to opt out - none of these count. The default cannot be "yes".
  • It must be evidenced. If you cannot produce a record of when and how the person consented, for enforcement purposes you effectively do not have consent.

LiveDeemed consent - the three ways it can arise3 min▶

Sometimes the law treats consent as given even without an explicit opt-in. This is deemed consent, and there are three routes. A leader does not need the section numbers, but should know these exist so the team neither over-claims nor ignores them.

  • By conduct: the person voluntarily provides their data for an obvious purpose - handing over a phone number to receive a delivery, for instance. Consent is deemed for that purpose only.
  • By contractual necessity: where sharing the data is reasonably necessary to fulfil a contract the person is party to.
  • By notification: you tell the person about a new purpose, do a required assessment that it is not likely to harm them, and give them a reasonable window to opt out. If they do not opt out, consent is deemed for that purpose.
The leadership read Deemed consent is real but narrow. It attaches to a specific purpose, not a blanket licence to market. "They gave us their number for a delivery" does not mean "we may text them promotions forever." When in doubt, get express consent - it is the cleanest thing to prove.
Self-studyConsent lets you reach a listed number - why that matters2 min read▶

Here is the point most teams miss: the register is not a wall. If a person has given you clear consent to send marketing to their number, you may message them even though that number sits on the Do Not Call Registry. Consent overrides the listing. This is why a well-run marketing operation invests in capturing and storing consent - it turns "we cannot touch listed numbers" into "we can reach everyone who actually said yes." The flip side: without consent, a listed number is off limits, full stop, and the check is what tells you whether a number is listed. Consent and the check are two tools for the same job - reaching a number lawfully - and you only need one of them for any given send.

Part 2 · covers what falls outside the DNC rules

The exemptions - what is out of scope 8 min live

The DNC rules only bite on a "specified message" - a message whose purpose is to market or promote. A whole category of messages never reaches that definition, because the law lists exclusions. If a message is on that list, you do not need consent and you do not need a register check. Knowing the list is how a leader stops the team wasting effort checking messages that were never in scope.

LiveThe ongoing-relationship exclusion (and a correction to teach)3 min▶

If you have an ongoing relationship with someone, a message that is on-topic to that relationship can fall outside the DNC rules. But the boundaries matter, and there is a common myth to correct.

  • It needs a real relationship. The PDPC looks for continuity, regularity, and frequency of dealings. A single one-off transaction is not an ongoing relationship.
  • The message must be on-topic. Reaching an existing customer about the product they already buy can qualify; using the relationship as a doorway to pitch something unrelated does not.
  • It covers all three channels. Under the current PDPA, this exclusion applies to voice calls, text messages, AND faxes. The older idea that it only covers text and fax but not voice calls is from the superseded pre-2019 framework - do not teach it.
Real world

Marina Retail: which messages need DNC compliance? Marina sends six kinds of message. An order-ready SMS (transactional) and a "your repair is done" text (service) are out of scope. A message to a supplier's procurement line about a bulk order (B2B) is out of scope. A satisfaction survey with no offer attached (market research) is out of scope. But the weekend-sale blast to past shoppers is a specified message - it needs consent or a register check. And a "we miss you, here is 20% off" to someone who bought once two years ago is not saved by "ongoing relationship" - one purchase long ago is not continuity, so it needs consent or a check too.

LiveTransactional, service, and B2B messages3 min▶

Three of the exclusions do most of the day-to-day work, because they cover the messages businesses send constantly. Knowing them keeps the team from over-checking.

  • Transactional and service messages: a message whose sole purpose is to carry out, complete, or confirm a transaction the person already agreed to, or to give service information - receipts, delivery updates, appointment reminders, account alerts. No selling attached.
  • B2B: a message sent to an organisation for the purposes of that organisation - not to an individual for their own use. Marketing to a company's general business line about a business product sits outside DNC.
  • The trap: the moment you bolt a promotion onto a transactional message ("your order shipped - and here is 30% off your next one"), the promotional part can pull it back into scope. Keep service and selling separate.
The leadership read Exemptions are about the purpose of the message, not who you are. There is no "we are a small shop so DNC does not apply." Ask of every template: what is this message actually for? If the honest answer is "to sell or promote," it is a specified message and the DNC rules apply.
Self-studyThe other exclusions, briefly2 min read▶

The Eighth Schedule lists the messages that are not specified messages. Alongside transactional/service, B2B, and ongoing-relationship, it covers: emergency messages (urgent safety or public need); market research and surveys, where you are gathering views rather than selling; personal or domestic messages sent by an individual in a private capacity; and public-agency non-commercial messages. The common thread is purpose - none of these exist to market or promote a product or service, so the Do Not Call machinery does not attach to them. For a leader, the value is not memorising all seven. It is recognising that a large share of the messages your organisation sends were never in scope, so the team's DNC energy should be aimed squarely at the ones that are: the promotional sends.

Consent, check, or exempt? ★ 12 min · pen and paper

Here are six messages Marina Retail wants to send. For each, decide the one thing that makes it lawful: do they already have consent, do they need a register check, or is it exempt (out of scope)? There is one best answer each.

Exercise 2 of 2

Draft Marina's consent line ★ 8 min · your next marketing review

Consent you cannot prove is not consent. Write the one-line opt-in statement Marina puts at sign-up - the sentence that turns a shopper into someone you may lawfully market to, even on a listed number.

Write a single sentence a customer actively agrees to. It must name who is sending, what (marketing messages), and which channels (calls / SMS / email).

Check it needs a positive action to accept - an unticked box the customer ticks, not a pre-ticked one. If your sentence works with silence, it fails.

Add how you will store the evidence: the date, the wording shown, and how they agreed. If you cannot answer "how would we prove this," rewrite it.

Read it aloud as a customer. If it feels sneaky, it is probably relying on inaction. Clear and unambiguous should also sound honest.

Why the wording earns its keep A good consent line is a business asset, not a legal formality. Every customer who opts in becomes someone you may reach even if their number is on the register - so the sentence that captures consent cleanly is quietly expanding your reachable audience, while protecting you if the PDPC ever asks how you got permission.
Homework

Before session a5 ◐ 30 min total

  • Pull three real marketing templates your organisation sends and label each: consent on file, needs a register check, or exempt. Note any you cannot classify.
  • Find your current sign-up consent wording. Test it against the "positive action, not silence" rule and mark whether it would survive.
  • Ask marketing whether any "transactional" message has a promotion bolted onto it - the most common way an exempt message quietly becomes a specified one.
  • Optional: skim the PDPC Advisory Guidelines on the Do Not Call Provisions to see the list of exclusions in the source itself.
★ Questions to ask your DPO and marketing team this week
  1. For our promotional sends, do we rely on consent or on register checks - and can we prove which, per campaign?
  2. Does our sign-up consent require a positive opt-in, or are we relying on pre-ticked boxes or people not opting out?
  3. Where do we store consent evidence, and could we produce it if the PDPC asked?
  4. Are we treating any message as "ongoing relationship" that is really just a single old transaction?
  5. Have we accidentally turned any transactional or service message into a specified message by adding a promotion?

Check yourself

Three questions before you go 🎯 ◐ 90 seconds

1 · A customer's number is on the Do Not Call Registry. You may still send them a marketing message if...

Consent overrides the listing. With clear consent on file you may message even a listed number; the register check is only needed when you do not have consent.

2 · Which of these counts as valid consent to market?

Consent needs a positive action and must be evidenced. Silence, inaction, and pre-ticked boxes are not consent.

3 · The ongoing-relationship exclusion, under the current PDPA...

The "text and fax only, not voice" version is the superseded pre-2019 framework. Today it covers all three channels, needs continuity and regularity, and the message must be on-topic to the relationship.
